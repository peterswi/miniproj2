Visualizations: Mini Project 2

Erin Corcoran, Lurein Perera, Will Peters & Kathryn Tatum

For our project, we decided to explore data on fatalities in police shootings in the United States since 2015. This data comes from the Washington Post's database on police shootings.

This is analysis of police killings in the US since 2015.

First, we wanted to know how many people were killed by police, and who they were. With this focus on the "who," we examined a variety of demographic data: age, gender, and race.
Our questions will appear above the visualizations that provide insight to them.

  • How many people have been killed by police since 2015?
  • Are these fatalities specific to a particlar region?

  2. Exploration

    3. When we were creating visualizations to show the locations where killings took place, Erin and Kathryn each created different ways to display the data. The visualizations ended up looking pretty similar at first glance -- with Erin's maps showing large dots where most killings were clustered, and Kathryn's showing individual dots for every killing. The main difference was the size of the individual dots. Both maps used semantic zoom, which is a form of zoom where the dots remain the exact same size when zooming in but appear further apart. Because Erin's visualization showed larger dots in the clustered areas, the dots representing individual killings are very small and hardly able to be seen -- especially when you zoom in. Although Kathryn's visualization had a better appearance when zooming in, we decided to use Erin's. We made this decision because we figured the way this visualization would be most used is by looking at the full national map to identify clusters. This experience taught us about the give and take which can be involved in choosing the right visualization.
